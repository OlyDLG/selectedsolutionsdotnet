Given the Balance Sheet and Income Statement data in the problem statement, calculate:

a) the accounting cash flow from operations;

Solution

This equals the Net profits after taxes entry in the Income Statement—$1,400—plus depreciation—$1,600—plus other noncash charges, which appear to be $0; thus the accounting cash flow from operations is simply $1,400 \(+\) $1,600 \(=\) $3,000.

b) the (financial) operating cash flow (OCF);

Solution

This equals the Earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT)—$2,700—minus taxes—$933—plus depreciation—$1,600—and thus is $2,700 \(-\) $933 \(+\) $1,600 \(=\) $3,367.

c) the free cash flow (FCF);

Solution

This is more complicated, as it requires a number of determinations of changes on the Balance Sheet from 2002 to 2003. To recap the text: FCF \(=\) OCF \(-\) NFAI \(-\) NCAI, where, in turn, NFAI \(=\) Change in net fixed assets \(+\) depreciation and NCAI \(=\) Change in current assets \(-\) Change in spontaneous current liabilities (SCL), this last, in turn, equaling Accounts payable + Accruals. The applicable Balance Sheet data are:

So, finally, the FCF = $3,367 \(-\) $1,400 \(-\) $1,400 \(=\) $567.

d) interpret, compare, and contrast the above results.

Solution

The $367 by which OCF is greater than the accounting cash flow from operations, reflects interest expense deducted from the latter but not the former. The $567 in FCF is the amount of cash available after operating income and expenses (other than interest) have been settled, i.e., it is what is available to cover interest and any non-operational expenses, e.g., paying dividends to shareholders, increasing the firm’s cash balance, etc. Since the interest commitment appears to be $367, that leaves $200 for such other non-operational expenses.

6) Project June, July, and August cash receipts, given the following sales and collections data: April: $65,000; May: $60,000; June: $70,000; July and August: $100,000 each; 50% collected in month zero, i.e., the month of the sale, and 25% collected in each of the following two months.

Solution

June receipts \(=\) 50% of June sales \(+\) 25% of May sales \(+\) 25% of April sales \(=\) 0.5($70,000) \(+\) 0.25($60,000) \(+\) 0.25($65,000) \(=\) $35,000 \(+\) $15,000 \(+\) $16,250 \(=\) $66,250.

Similarly, July receipts \(=\) $50,000 \(+\) $17,500 \(+\) $15,000 \(=\) $82,500; August receipts \(=\) $50,000 \(+\) $25,000 \(+\) $17,500 \(=\) $92,500.

14) Prepare a pro forma Income Statement (Part a) and Balance Sheet (Part b), and determine the EFR (Part c) for Red Queen Restaurants for 2004, based on the data given in the problem statement. (Again, unless marked otherwise, dollar values in tables are data given in the problem statement and are times 1,000.)

Solution:

First note that the stipulation in the problem statement that ending inventory and accounts payable change directly with sales means that the change in one is proportional to the change in the other. Consequently, since sales is forecast to increase by 12.5% ( ($900,000 \(-\) $800,000) / $800,000 = 0.125), our 2004 forecast ending inventory and accounts payable are both 0.125 * $100,000 \(=\) $12,500 over their 2003 values, i.e., $112,500. Additionally, from 2003, the cost of goods sold percent of sales is $600,000 / $800,000 = 0.75 and the operating expenses percent of sales is $100,000 / $800,000 = 0.125.
