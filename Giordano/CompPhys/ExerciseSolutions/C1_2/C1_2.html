Exercise 1.4

\(~~~~\)Use the Euler method to solve the system:\[ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{dN_A}{dt} & = & -&\frac{N_A}{\tau_A} & & (\text{Eq. 1})\\ \frac{dN_B}{dt} & = & &\frac{N_A}{\tau_A} - \frac{N_B}{\tau_B} & & (\text{Eq. 2}) \end{eqnarray} \] Also find the exact solution and explore the behavior for different values of the ratio \(\tau_A/\tau_B\).

Solution

\(~~~~\)To solve this system exactly, first observe that Eq. 1 doesn’t depend on \(N_B\) or its derivative, and thus \(N_A\) can be solved for independently of \(N_B\); that solution is: \[N_A(t) = A_0 e^{-\alpha t}\] where \(A_0 = N_A(0)\) and \(\displaystyle\alpha = \frac1{\tau_A}\). (Check: \(\frac d{dt}A_0 e^{-\alpha t} = A_0(-\alpha) e^{-\alpha t} = -\alpha N_A = -N_A/\tau_A\); \(N_A(0) = A_0 e^{-\alpha (0)} = A_0 \checkmark\).) We substitute this in for \(N_A\) in Eq. 2 (and rename \(\displaystyle \frac1{\tau_B} = \beta\)), yielding: \[\frac{dN_B}{dt} = \alpha A_0 e^{-\alpha t} - \beta N_B \implies \frac{dN_B}{dt} + \beta N_B = \alpha A_0 e^{-\alpha t}.\] This first-order linear inhomogeneous ODE no longer depends (explicitly) on \(N_A\), and thus it is solvable exactly for \(N_B\) by finding a suitable integrating factor. (There are several serviceable presentations of this method available on the internet; the reader unfamiliar with this method is encouraged to search for it.) The exact solution is: \[N_B(t) = B_0 e^{-\beta t} + \frac{\alpha A_0}{\beta - \alpha} \left(e^{-\alpha t} - e^{-\beta t} \right)\] where \(B_0 = N_B(0)\). (Check: \(\frac d{dt}N_B + \beta N_B = \cancel{-\beta B_0 e^{-\beta t}} - \frac{\alpha^2 A_0}{\beta - \alpha} e^{-\alpha t} + \cancel{\frac{\alpha\beta A_0}{\beta - \alpha} e^{-\beta t}} + \cancel{\beta B_0e^{-\beta t}} + \frac{\alpha \beta A_0}{\beta -\alpha}e^{-\alpha t} - \cancel{\frac{\alpha \beta A_0}{\beta -\alpha}e^{-\beta t}}=\) \(\frac{\cancel{\beta - \alpha}}{\cancel{\beta - \alpha}} \alpha A_0 e^{-\alpha t}\checkmark, N_B(0) = B_0(1) + \frac{\alpha A_0}{\beta - \alpha} (1-1) = B_0\checkmark\).)

These exact solutions are plotted below as smooth curves, along with the Euler method solutions for 100 intervals (all solutions span \(t= 0\) to \(10.0\)) and a user-selectable number of intervals (10, 20, 30, 40, or 50); \(1/\tau_A\) and \(1/\tau_B\) are also user-selectable; \(N_A(0) = 100\) and \(N_B(0) = 0\) are fixed.

\(~~~~\)In the next chapter, the authors emphasize that their primary concern for the text is physics (as opposed to numerical methods and/or programming). With that in mind, the reader should use the interactivity provided to observe and understand three key phenomena. First, the qualitative behavior of both solutions, both independently (i.e., the effect of changing \(1/\tau_A\) on \(N_A\) and the effect of changing \(1/\tau_B\) on \(N_B\) while leaving \(1/\tau_A\) unchanged) and relative to one another (i.e., the effect of changing \(1/\tau_A\) on \(N_B\) and the (lack of) effect on \(N_A\) when changing \(1/\tau_B\)). Second, the fact that the numerical solution can (badly) misrepresent the physics if the independent variable resolution (i.e., the number of intervals) is insufficient (e.g., the results when the alternate number of intervals is 10); but, third, what constitutes sufficient resolution can (usually will) depend on the physical parameters of the situation, e.g., in this case, \(1/\tau_A\) and \(1/\tau_B\). (For example, observe that, for larger values of those parameters—corresponding to faster decay—\(n=10\) results in completely unphysical solutions, but for slow decay—smaller parameter values—\(n=10\) is sufficient to give qualitatively correct results; the reader should spend as much time as required to understand this dichotomy.)

Exercise 1.5

\(~~~~\)Use the Euler method to solve the system: \[ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{dN_A}{dt} & = & \frac{N_B}{\tau} -\frac{N_A}{\tau} & & (\text{Eq. 1})\\ \frac{dN_B}{dt} & = & \frac{N_A}{\tau} - \frac{N_B}{\tau} & & (\text{Eq. 2})\\ N_A(0) & = & A_0\\ N_B(0) & = & B_0 \end{eqnarray} \] Let \(\tau = 1\) and explore the behavior for different values of \(A_0\) and \(B_0\).

Solution

\(~~~~\)Although not mentioned in the Exercise statement, this system too can be solved exactly and, again, methods for doing so are readily available on the internet; and again, we provide and graph, as smooth curves, the exact solution for comparison to our Euler method results. First note that, with the stipulation to take \(\tau = 1\), the system becomes simply: \[\begin{eqnarray} \frac{dN_A}{dt} & = & N_B - N_A & & (\text{Eq. 1})\\ \frac{dN_B}{dt} & = & N_A - N_B & & (\text{Eq. 2})\\ N_A(0) & = & A_0\\ N_B(0) & = & B_0 \end{eqnarray} \] The exact solution is: \[\begin{eqnarray} N_A(t) & = & \frac12\left[(A_0 - B_0)e^{-2t} + A_0 + B_0\right]\\ N_B(t) & = & \frac12\left[(B_0 - A_0)e^{-2t} + A_0 + B_0\right] \end{eqnarray} \] (Check: \(\frac d{dt}N_A = \frac12(-2)(A_0 - B_0)e^{-2t} = (B_0 - A_0)e^{-2t} = \frac12(2B_0 - 2A_0)e^{-2t}= N_B - N_A,\) \(N_A(0) = \frac12(A_0 - B_0 + A_0 + B_0) = A_0\checkmark\); the exactly analogous check for \(N_B\) is left to the reader.)

\(~~~~\)As with Exercise 1.4, the reader should study and come to understand the effects of changing each of the adjustable parameters below.

Exercise 2.5

\(~~~~\)Modify the bicycle air resistance model to use constant force at low velocity, switching over to the given constant power model at some specified transition velocity.

Solution

\(~~~~\)We’ve plotted four curves below: the red and blue curves are the constant-power (cp) model with and without drag, respectively (we’ve plotted these as a check on our implementation); the yellow curve is the asked-for constant-force (cf) at low speed, cp at high speed model; the green curve is extra and requires some explanation.

\(~~~~\)It’s not very physical to transition abruptly between these two models: outside of quantum scales, nature abhors discontinuities. The simplest—and not uncommon—way to address this is to transition linearly from one model to the other. Let \(\varphi_{\text{model}}(\upsilon)\) be the (non-drag) coefficient multiplying \(dt\), i.e., \(\varphi_{\text{cf}}(\upsilon) = F_0/m\) and \(\varphi_{\text{cp}}(\upsilon) = P/(m\upsilon)\), and define \(\displaystyle \alpha = \frac{\upsilon~}{\upsilon^*}\); then \(\varphi_{\text{lt}}(\upsilon) = (1-\alpha)\varphi_{\text{cf}}(\upsilon) + \alpha\varphi_{\text{cp}}(\upsilon)\) gives a linear transition between the two coefficients that equals \(\varphi_{\text{cf}}\) when \(\upsilon = 0\) and \(\varphi_{\text{cp}}\) when \(\upsilon = \upsilon^*\). Moreover, \(\alpha\varphi_{\text{cp}}(\upsilon) = \displaystyle \left(\frac{\cancel{\upsilon}}{\upsilon^*}\right)\left(\frac{P}{m\cancel{\upsilon}}\right) = \frac{P}{m\upsilon^*}\), so actually \[\varphi_{\text{lt}}(\upsilon) = (1-\alpha)\frac{F_0}{m} + \frac{P}{m\upsilon^*},\] i.e., \(\varphi_{\text{lt}}\) is well-defined for \(\upsilon = 0\). (This is fortunate because otherwise we would still have to treat the \(\upsilon = 0\) case separately.) The green curve below uses this \(\varphi_{\text{lt}}\) in place of simply \(\varphi_{\text{cf}}\) for \(0 \le \upsilon_i \lt \upsilon^*\), and then switches to (just) \(\varphi_{\text{cp}}\) for \(\upsilon \ge \upsilon^*\). (This switch to just the cp model is still necessary because when \(\upsilon \gt \upsilon^*,~\alpha\gt 1 \implies \varphi_{\text{lt}}\) includes too much \(\varphi_{\text{cp}}\) and a negative (!) amount of \(\varphi_{\text{cf}}\). Note that, consequently, some discontinuity in going from one model to the other remains, but it is less than that resulting from an abrupt change from a constant coefficient to a 1-over-\(\upsilon\) coefficient).

\(~~~~\)We have also included the ability to choose the speed at which the transition takes place, so that the user can better appreciate the physics of this parameter.

Exercise 2.14

\(~~~~\)Add a crosswind to the fly ball (hit baseball) model; determine how it affects the landing spot.

Solution

\(~~~~\)We are going to solve the general case, giving the reader five (horizontal) wind directions to choose from: tailwind, 45° cross-tailwind, 90° crosswind, 45° cross-headwind, and headwind; \(x\) continues to denote the (fixed) initial horizontal direction of the batted ball, \(y\) continues to denote its altitude, and the new coordinate \(z\)—listed third in our ordered-triple vectors—denotes the horizontal orthogonal to \(x\), i.e., the lateral displacement. The ball’s velocity vector (whose magnitude is denoted simply by \(\upsilon\)) is \(\vec{\upsilon} = (u, v, w)\); the (constant) wind vector (speed \(W\) user-selectable) is \(\vec{W} = (W_x, 0, W_z) = (W\cos\phi, 0, W\sin\phi)\), \(\phi\) such that a tailwind corresponds to \(\phi=0\). The initial position \((x_0, y_0, z_0)\), in meters, is \((0, 1, 0)\); the initial velocity is \((\upsilon_0\cos\theta, \upsilon_0\sin\theta, 0)\), where \(\upsilon_0\) is the initial speed (converted to meters per second from user-selectable miles per hour) and (user-selectable) \(\theta\) is the launch angle. For the time derivative we use Newton’s over-dot notation, e.g., \(dx/dt = \dot{x}\).

\(~~~~\)With these notational conventions, our model is:

\[\begin{eqnarray} \dot{x} & = & u, & x_0 = 0 \\ \dot{y} & = & v, & y_0 = 1 \\ \dot{z} & = & w, & z_0 = 0 \\ \dot{u} & = & -\frac{B_2}{m}\left|\vec{\upsilon}-\vec{W}\right|(u-W_x), & u_0 = \upsilon_0\cos\theta \\ \dot{v} & = & -\frac{B_2}{m}\upsilon v - g, & v_0 = \upsilon_0\sin\theta \\ \dot{w} & = & -\frac{B_2}{m}\left|\vec{\upsilon}-\vec{W}\right|(w-W_z), & w_0 = 0 \\ \end{eqnarray} \]

where \(\displaystyle \frac{B_2}{m} = 0.0039 + \frac{0.0058}{1+\exp\left[\left(\left|\vec{\upsilon}-\vec{W}\right|-35\right)/5\right]}\) is given in the text. Discretized this becomes:

\[\begin{eqnarray} x_{n+1} & = & x_n + u_n\Delta t \\ y_{n+1} & = & y_n + v_n\Delta t \\ z_{n+1} & = & z_n + w_n\Delta t \\ u_{n+1} & = & u_n -\frac{{B_2}_{n}}{m}\left|\vec{\upsilon}_n-\vec{W}\right|(u_n-W_x)\Delta t \\ v_{n+1} & = & v_n - \left(\frac{{B_2}_{n}}{m}\upsilon_n v_n + g\right)\Delta t \\ w_{n+1} & = & w_n -\frac{{B_2}_{n}}{m}\left|\vec{\upsilon}_n-\vec{W}\right|(w_n-W_z)\Delta t \\ \end{eqnarray} \]

with the same initial conditions. \(y\) vs. \(x\) and \(z\) vs. \(x\) trajectories are plotted below, and below that we give the time aloft, the primary horizontal range, and the lateral displacement (these are taken from the last \(t, x,\&~z\) values before the first negative \(y\) value—given our time step of 0.1 second, we regard these as close enough.) Note the lateral displacement from even just a mild 5 mph (8 kph) 45° crosswind: this author was surprised by its magnitude.

Exercise 2.21

\(~~~~\)Calculate the trajectory of a batted ball with side spin, i.e., angular velocity pseudovector parallel to the vertical axis.

Solution

\(~~~~\)In the model given in the text for a thrown ball with side spin, the neglecting of drag in the \(y~\&~z\) directions and of spin in the \(x\) direction is justified (implicitly) by the comparatively short flight time and the comparatively negligible initial vertical component. However, from having solved Exercise 2.14, we know, or at least should suspect, that the larger initial vertical velocity component and the longer flight time might make these neglections unsupportable. Thus we will include drag\(^1\) in all three velocity component equations, and include the general Magnus force—given in text footnote 22 to be \(\vec{F}_M = S_0\vec{\omega}\times \vec{\upsilon}\), where \(\times\) denotes the vector cross product—in both horizontal directions. (Reader: why don’t we have to consider the effect of \(\vec{F}_M\) on the vertical movement? Hint: what’s the vertical component of \(\vec{\omega}\times \vec{\upsilon}\) if \(\vec{\omega}\) is purely vertical?)

\(~~~~\)Now, the cross product is an oriented operation: \(\vec{u} \times \vec{v} = -(\vec{v} \times \vec{u})\), i.e., order matters. Consequently, as a guard against making careless mistakes, in this Exercise, we are going to assign \(x, y, z~\&~u, v, w\) their more customary roles: \(x\) still denotes the initial horizontal direction of flight, but now \(y\) denotes the lateral horizontal displacement, and \(z\) represents the vertical position (and \(u, v, w\) represent their respective derivatives). With this as our coordinate system, our continuous model is:

\[\begin{eqnarray} \dot{x} & = & u, & x_0 = 0 \\ \dot{y} & = & v, & y_0 = 0 \\ \dot{z} & = & w, & z_0 = 1 \\ \dot{u} & = & -\frac{B_2}{m}\upsilon u - \frac{S_0}{m}(\vec{\omega} \times \vec{\upsilon})_x, & u_0 = \upsilon_0\cos\theta \\ \dot{v} & = & -\frac{B_2}{m}\upsilon v - \frac{S_0}{m}(\vec{\omega} \times \vec{\upsilon})_y, & v_0 = 0 \\ \dot{w} & = & -\frac{B_2}{m}\upsilon w - g, & w_0 = \upsilon_0\sin\theta \\ \end{eqnarray} \]

where, as in the text, \(\displaystyle\frac{S_0}{m} = 4.1\times 10^{-4}, \frac{B_2}{m}\) is as in Exercise 2.14, and \(\vec{\omega} = (0,0,\omega), \omega\) user-selectable. Discretized, this becomes:

\[\begin{eqnarray} x_{n+1} & = & x_n + u_n\Delta t \\ y_{n+1} & = & y_n + v_n\Delta t \\ z_{n+1} & = & z_n + w_n\Delta t \\ u_{n+1} & = & u_n -\left(\frac{{B_2}_{n}}{m}\upsilon_n u_n + \frac{S_0}{m}(\vec{\omega} \times \vec{\upsilon}_n)_x\right)\Delta t \\ v_{n+1} & = & v_n - \left(\frac{{B_2}_{n}}{m}\upsilon_n v_n + \frac{S_0}{m}(\vec{\omega} \times \vec{\upsilon}_n)_y\right)\Delta t \\ w_{n+1} & = & w_n - \left(\frac{{B_2}_{n}}{m}\upsilon_n w_n + g\right)\Delta t \\ \end{eqnarray} \]

Results are plotted below.
